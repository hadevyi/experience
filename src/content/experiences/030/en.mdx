  overview: |
    This was a manual and automatic background removal editor and performance evaluation project completed during my ETRI internship.

    The core idea was to separate foreground and background in the first frame of a video and apply that reference to the full video. We worked on both a manual editor for correcting the background and an Autoencoder-based automatic background removal experiment, followed by performance evaluation.
  background: |
    The main keyword of the internship task was interactive media.

    We explored how foreground/background information from the first frame of a video could be applied to later frames. To do that, we prepared both a human-correctable editor and an automation experiment.
  scope: |
    The project scope was divided into a manual editor, automatic background removal experiments, and performance evaluation.

    In the manual editor, we loaded video frames, corrected foreground and background areas with a brush, moved between frames, selected colors, saved results, extracted video frames, and rebuilt video output. In the automatic background removal experiment, we used the first-frame background information as training data and applied the result to the full video.
    Since automatic results needed a performance baseline, we also structured the manually corrected output as a reference for comparison.
  contribution: |
    As a two-person team, we completed the implementation scope discussed for the project.

    We implemented a Python-based desktop editor, used OpenCV GrabCut for background correction, ran Keras Autoencoder experiments for automatic background removal, generated mask videos, and evaluated results with F-measure. The final result was summarized in a short two-page paper.
  roleDetails:
    - Implemented the manual background removal editor UI and editing flow with Tkinter and Pillow.
    - Implemented video frame extraction, mask processing, and video saving with OpenCV.
    - Ran Keras Autoencoder experiments based on the corrected first-frame result.
    - Built an F-measure based evaluation flow to compare automatic output with manual reference videos.
    - Summarized the project results in a short two-page paper.
  implementation: |
    The manual editor was built with a Tkinter UI and OpenCV/Pillow image processing flow.
    It included brush size control, foreground/background color selection, frame navigation, Undo/Redo, video cutting, and save flows.

    The automatic background removal experiment used the foreground/background separation result from the first frame as training data, then applied a Keras Autoencoder to later video frames. We compared experiments that split frames into 10x10, 20x20, and 40x40 units. For performance evaluation, we generated mask videos and compared manual and automatic outputs.
  challenges: |
    I had to study Python and AI models while applying them directly to the task.

    Video processing was harder than working with a single image because consistency across frames mattered. It was also difficult to improve the CNN/Autoencoder logic in a GPU environment and decide how to lower loss, because results changed depending on each case and the quality of the video.

    At first, the goal was closer to building a generally effective separation model, but that scope was too broad to validate reliably within the internship period. We narrowed the goal to optimizing around one frame of one video and applying that reference to the full video.
  outcome: |
    We implemented the manual/automatic background removal editor and performance evaluation flow, then summarized the results in a short two-page paper.

    In the experiment, we compared 10x10, 20x20, and 40x40 units. F-measure was measured at 0.9614, 0.9702, and 0.9787, while loss decreased to 0.0035, 0.0020, and 0.0014 respectively. Training time increased to 420 seconds, 1014 seconds, and 1320 seconds, so we compared both output quality and training cost.

    In the paper, we compared the manually corrected reference with the automated experimental output and summarized the before-and-after results and limitations.

    Through this project, I applied Python, OpenCV, and Keras to a real task and experienced how AI could be used experimentally in image processing and video editing.
