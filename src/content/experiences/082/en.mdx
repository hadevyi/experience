teachingMentoring:
  audience: |
    I mentored two teams, each consisting of four to five students.

    The teams came from the Drone IT department and the Computer Science department, and the program helped them try a lightweight project flow during vacation.
  reason: |
    I joined after a request from a university professor.

    It was a special vacation mentoring program outside the regular curriculum, intended to help students understand the basic flow needed to start a project.
  content: |
    I structured the four sessions around an introductory project experience.

    - Key artifacts created during a project
    - How to document project experience
    - Basic Git usage flow
    - Open Q&A based on students' questions
  approach: |
    The mentoring was conducted online, once a week for one hour per team, across four sessions.

    Rather than focusing only on completion, I organized the sessions around reusable foundations such as artifacts, documentation, and collaboration tools.
  response: |
    Some students already knew about me even though I had graduated some time ago, and they shared that they were glad to meet and ask questions.

    Many had not experienced projects outside school, so they said it was helpful to hear practical project stories. During the mentoring, each student created a Git account and started managing it.
  learning: |
    This experience was meaningful because I had also received a lot of mentoring support during my undergraduate years.

    It reminded me that, for project beginners, helping them understand how to start, document, and collaborate can matter more than introducing advanced technical topics.
