---
title: Teaching Assistant at the Gifted Education Center
organization: Inje University Gifted Education Center
summary: A TA activity where I taught development basics to middle school students in an information science program and gradually took on more responsibility for classes and project feedback.
oneLine: TA activity supporting information science classes and student projects
highlights:
  - Supported biweekly Saturday classes and intensive vacation sessions
  - Assisted classes on Android, Python, algorithms, and introductory AI
  - Supported small-group project feedback and completion
teachingMentoring:
  audience: |
    I worked with middle school students in the information science program at the Inje University Gifted Education Center. Classes were held every other Saturday, with intensive week-long sessions during vacation periods.

    The classes were small: around five to six students in the advanced class and three students in the mentorship/project class. Because of that, it was important to closely follow each student's understanding and project progress rather than simply assisting the class from a distance.
  reason: |
    This activity naturally came through my life in Database LAB. It was originally a part-time role handled by lab members, and I joined after being recommended while spending time in the lab.

    It felt meaningful to share what I had learned in school with younger students. At first, I mainly supported senior lab members, but over time I took on more direct responsibility for classes and feedback.
  content: |
    In 2017, I supported senior students who were already leading the classes. I mainly handled attendance, answered questions, supported Android classes, and gave feedback on final projects.

    In 2018, I became more directly responsible for the advanced class and mainly taught basic Python. Since Python was not a language I had learned through coursework, I studied it on my own and prepared example code and presentation materials for the students.

    In 2019, my work with the advanced class naturally extended into supporting the mentorship/project students as well. I gave feedback on Python-based projects, introduced basic AI concepts, and followed the students through to completion.
  approach: |
    Because I was explaining development concepts to middle school students, I focused on turning what I knew into examples and flows they could actually follow. Rather than giving long explanations, I tried to prepare runnable code and short exercises.

    Since the classes were small, I could closely observe where each student was stuck and how their projects were progressing. During project feedback, I tried to help students find their next step instead of simply giving them the answer.
  response: |
    I still occasionally keep in touch with some of the students I met through this activity. Some of them are still developing, which made me realize that what I shared could remain meaningful for someone over time.

    This experience stayed with me because it was not only about assisting a class, but also about becoming a small starting point in someone else's exploration of development.
  learning: |
    I learned that teaching is not just saying what I know. It is the process of reorganizing knowledge into a form the listener can understand. Studying Python on my own and turning it into class material also became practice in quickly learning and explaining new topics.

    Later, when preparing major-experience sessions for high school students and other mentoring activities, I began to think first about the listener's age, background, and level of understanding. This TA activity became the starting point for how I learned to teach and mentor.
