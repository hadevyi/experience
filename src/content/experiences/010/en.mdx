---
title: Major Experience Representative for High School Students
organization: Inje University Admissions Office
summary: A lecture and mentoring activity introducing Computer Engineering to high school students considering their future major.
oneLine: A representative activity that shared perspectives on choosing Computer Engineering
highlights:
  - Introduced Computer Engineering to high school students
  - Shared perspectives on choosing a school and major
  - Led basic Python practice and career counseling
teachingMentoring:
  audience: |
    This activity was for first- and second-year high school students who were interested in Computer Engineering and had voluntarily applied for the program. I was told that the students were not simply attending a required school event, but had chosen to participate because they wanted to learn more about the major.

    Some sessions were held at high schools, while others were held for students visiting the university for a major experience program. Depending on the session, the format included a lecture, major introduction, short practice activity, and counseling.
  reason: |
    I joined after being recommended by the department office. I did not even know this kind of representative activity existed, but once I received the recommendation, it felt meaningful because I had also spent a lot of time thinking about which university and major to choose.

    Because I had also hesitated a lot in front of that decision, I could naturally empathize with the students' questions. I participated with the hope that sharing my own concerns and choices might help even one student feel less uncertain.
  content: |
    I talked about how to choose a school, how to think about choosing a major, and what Computer Engineering studies and works with. I tried to explain that Computer Engineering is not just about being good with computers, but about learning how to structure and solve problems.

    I also included a short Python coding exercise so the students could experience a small part of the field directly. I wanted them to do more than just listen to an explanation, even if the practice was brief.
  approach: |
    Because the audience was high school students, I focused less on technical depth and more on questions they were likely to have while thinking about their future path. I first thought about what I would have wanted to know as a high school student, and what kind of explanation could make the decision feel less vague.

    The session connected perspectives on choosing a school and major, an introduction to what students learn in Computer Engineering, and a simple practice flow. After the lecture and practice session, I also made space for individual conversations so students could ask more personal questions.
  response: |
    Some students asked for my contact information afterward. Some of them were seriously considering Computer Engineering and wanted to continue the conversation through more personal counseling.

    It stayed with me that a major introduction could become a concrete starting point for someone else's decision. This was also when I first felt that my own past concerns could become a small reference point for someone else.
  learning: |
    This experience became the starting point for how I think about preparing lectures for the audience in front of me. Since then, when preparing lectures or mentoring sessions, I have tried to first consider what information the audience needs and what level of explanation they can follow.

    I learned that it is more important to consider what the listener needs and can follow than to simply deliver what I want to say. This activity was the beginning of learning how to prepare not just the lecture I wanted to give, but the lecture someone actually needed.
